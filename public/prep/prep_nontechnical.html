02 · Call Format

What This Call Actually Is

A 30-minute CISO intro is almost never a technical screen. Treat it as a thinking and fit assessment. The CISO wants to know how you reason, not whether you can recite CVEs.

This call IS
  • A test of how you think about security
  • A motivation and culture check
  • Them selling the role to you too
  • An opportunity to show company research
  • A conversation, not an exam
This call is NOT
  • A technical deep-dive
  • A CTF or exploit walkthrough
  • A resume recitation session
  • A trick — be direct and honest
  • 30 minutes of you talking

Pacing Advice

  • Answer questions in 60–90 seconds. Stop. Let them respond.
  • Have 2–3 questions ready to ask. Don't ask all of them.
  • If you don't know something, say so and pivot to what you do know.
  • Don't oversell anything. CISOs have seen every resume trope — directness lands better.
03 · Motivation Questions

Why Strike, Why This Role

"Tell me about yourself."
"I've spent most of my career finding the gap between how security teams operate and how engineering teams actually build things — and trying to close that gap from the inside rather than from a review queue. At Bureau Veritas I was doing security reviews of AWS services, but the part that stuck was when engineering teams started pulling me into design discussions before code was written, rather than after. That shift — from reviewer to collaborator — is what I want to build on. This role at Strike is explicitly that. That's why I'm here."
Don't walk through your resume chronologically. One thread, two examples max, stop.
"Why Strike specifically?"
"A few reasons. The BitLicense is recent — Strike just came under NYDFS oversight in March. That means there's real, immediate security work to build out, not a backlog of theoretical improvements. I find that more interesting than joining a mature program where the foundations are already set. And the consequence of failure here is concrete — customer funds — which makes security prioritisation decisions cleaner than at companies where the risk is more abstract. The stakes are legible."
Shows company research, honest reasoning. Avoid saying "I love Bitcoin" unless you genuinely do.
"What does good AppSec embedded in engineering look like to you?"
"It means being in the RFC before the design is finalised, not reviewing code after it ships. The goal is to get engineers reasoning about threat surfaces themselves — not to replace that reasoning. In practice that means your opinion needs to be trusted and fast. If you're a bottleneck or a gatekeeper, you've already lost the collaboration. The best outcome is when a team doesn't need to ask you because they've internalized the right questions."
"How do you handle pushback from engineering on a security finding?"
"Most pushback is legitimate. Engineers have deadlines, and not every finding is equally urgent. I try to frame findings in terms of realistic exploit scenarios and business consequence rather than CVSS scores — because a 9.8 that's unreachable is less urgent than a 6.5 on a payment endpoint. The goal is a shared risk decision, not a security veto. If we disagree after that, I escalate. But that's rarely where it lands."
04 · Questions to Ask

What to Ask the CISO

Pick 2–3. Don't ask all of them. The goal is a real conversation, not a list.

  • "The role was initiated to bridge security and engineering — what's the current gap you're trying to close?"
    Gets them talking about the actual problem. Tells you what success looks like from their side.
  • "With the NY BitLicense, how is NYDFS oversight shaping the security roadmap right now?"
    Shows you did research. Signals you understand the regulatory pressure driving the hire.
  • "What does success look like for this hire in the first 90 days?"
    Standard but essential. Their answer tells you the real priorities vs. the JD's aspirational list.
  • "How mature is the security program today — are you building foundations or refining something that already exists?"
    At a Series B company this answer shapes everything. You need to know if you're building from scratch.
  • "How does the engineering team currently think about security — is it something they pull you in for, or something they're expected to own themselves?"
    Tells you how much culture change is needed vs. process improvement.
05 · Culture Fit

The Bitcoin Question

Strike is ideologically Bitcoin-first. Jack Mallers is vocal about it. You don't need to be a Bitcoin maximalist — but you should be able to say why securing a financial product appeals to you without stumbling on the Bitcoin context.

If asked "does the Bitcoin focus matter to you?"

"What matters to me is that the product is consequential. With Strike, a security failure has a direct, legible impact — customer funds. That makes the work feel real in a way that abstract data breach risk doesn't always. Whether the underlying asset is Bitcoin or dollars, the security engineering is serious and the stakes are clear. I find that more motivating than the alternative."
Honest. Doesn't fake enthusiasm you might not have. Respects the company without overselling.
06 · Market Context

The Down Market Conversation

Where BTC actually is: Around $68K as of early April 2026. That sounds reasonable until you know it peaked at ~$126K. Q1 2026 was rough — January down 10%, February down 15%, March barely flat. Fear & Greed index at 8 out of 100. Extreme Fear. The macro backdrop is an Iran conflict driving oil prices up, inflation worries, and a Fed sitting on its hands.

Why it matters for Strike: Their lending product (BTC-backed loans, launched May 2025) was built when BTC was significantly higher. Borrowers who took loans against BTC at $100K+ are sitting on collateral that's lost nearly half its value. Liquidation risk is real — and that's both a business problem and an engineering and security problem.

Questions the CISO Might Ask You

"We're in a down market. Why do you want to join a Bitcoin company right now?"
"Honestly, a down market is when security matters more, not less. When things are going well, people tolerate process debt. When revenue is under pressure, the cost of an incident — reputational or financial — is higher. The security engineering I'd be doing isn't contingent on BTC price. The attack surface doesn't shrink when the price does."
Subtext: are you here because you couldn't get a job elsewhere? Don't get defensive. Answer through the lens of the work, not the market.
"How do you prioritize security investment when budgets are tight?"
"I'd start with what's existential versus what's important. For Strike, an authentication failure or a custody breach in a down market could be catastrophic — both financially and reputationally. That stays at the top regardless of budget. Below that, I'd focus on things that scale — tooling, detection rules, getting engineers to own security checks themselves — rather than labour-intensive manual processes that only work if you have headcount. The goal is making the security program cheaper to maintain over time, not just adding controls."
Series B in a bear market means the CISO is likely working with less than they'd like. Show you can do more with less.
"Our lending product is under stress right now. How do you think about security in a high-pressure operational environment?"
"High-pressure moments are exactly when security regressions happen. Engineers move fast to fix a business problem and skip the review. I'd want to be close enough to the engineering team that when something like a liquidation event creates operational pressure, I'm already in the room — not being looped in after the fact. The security risk in a down market isn't just external attackers. It's rushed code and skipped reviews when the business is stressed."
This is a sophisticated question. It shows they trust you enough to be real about the product stress. Match that directness.
"Are you comfortable with the instability of the crypto industry?"
"I've thought about that. I'm not here because crypto is the only option — I'm here because the security problem at a regulated financial product is genuinely interesting to work on. The regulatory environment, the attack surface, the stakes — these are things I want to build experience in. A down market doesn't change that."
They're really asking: will you leave in six months when a FAANG calls? Stability of intent is the signal, not stability of the market.

Questions You Should Ask the CISO

  • "How is the lending product performing operationally in the current market — are there security concerns you're watching more closely as BTC prices are down?"
    Sharp question. Shows you understand the business risk, not just the technical attack surface. Most candidates won't ask it. Also gives you real intelligence on what you'd be walking into.
  • "Has the security roadmap shifted at all given Q1 — any priorities that got accelerated or deprioritized?"
    Down markets cause reprioritization. Knowing whether security investment is protected or being cut tells you a lot about how seriously they take it.
  • "Is this headcount fully approved and budgeted?"
    Blunt, but fair. At a Series B in a bear market, headcount freezes happen. Frame it cleanly: "I want to be respectful of both our time — is this role fully approved, or is it still contingent on anything?"
  • "How does Strike think about the security implications of BTC collateral moving to third-party capital providers during the lending process — that's a meaningful trust boundary."
    Shows you read the product in detail. The custody handoff to lending partners is exactly the kind of third-party risk the CISO should have a view on.

The Meta-Point

A CISO at a Bitcoin company in a down market is under real pressure. Revenue is softer, the board is watching costs, regulatory requirements are only increasing. The person who gets hired in this environment is the one who makes the CISO feel the security program will be in safer hands — not the one who asks for more resources. Frame everything through: I can do more with less, I know how to prioritize, and I understand that security debt in a down market has higher consequences than in a bull run.
07 · Blockchain Learning

The Smart Contract Course

The real story: At Bureau Veritas, curiosity about where financial infrastructure was heading led you to start Johnny Cash's Smart Contract Hacking course. You got through the fundamentals — Solidity basics, how the EVM works, fungible and non-fungible token standards — then project overload at BV meant you had to pause. You haven't resumed it yet.

Don't dress it up. The honest version is the right version. For the actual concepts, see the Blockchain Prep doc.

How to Say It

If the course comes up in conversation
"During my time at BV I started getting curious about blockchain security — it felt like a natural direction given how much financial infrastructure is moving that way. I started a smart contract hacking course, got through the fundamentals — Solidity, how the EVM works, token standards — and then a run of projects meant I had to put it down. It's on my list to pick back up. What I did get through was enough to understand why the security model is genuinely different. Immutability changes the cost of a missed finding in a way that sharpens how you think about severity."
Honest about the pause. Ends on a conceptual point rather than a credential claim.
