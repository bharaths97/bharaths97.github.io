What these questions are really testing: Not whether you know Strike's stack. They're testing whether you'd show up as a collaborator or a reviewer. Every answer should sound like someone who was already in the room when the design was being made — not someone who was handed the output to assess. Lead with questions. Say "we." Anchor to the design phase, not the audit phase.
01
Threat Modeling

Walk me through how you'd threat model [feature]

"Walk me through how you'd threat model our Lightning payment flow." ▾
Lightning payments: user initiates payment → Strike node routes over Lightning Network → recipient receives BTC. Near-instant, low-fee. Multi-hop routing through third-party nodes.
How to think through it
  • Who are the actors? Authenticated user, Strike's Lightning node, intermediate routing nodes (third-party, untrusted), recipient.
  • What are the trust boundaries? User → Strike API (authenticated, rate-limited). Strike node → Lightning Network (cryptographic but routing nodes are untrusted). Strike → recipient (payment finality, no reversal).
  • What's the highest-value target? Channel funds. Routing manipulation. Payment state confusion — paying the wrong recipient, double-spend edge cases.
  • STRIDE pass: Spoofing (can someone impersonate a routing node?), Tampering (can payment state be manipulated mid-route?), Repudiation (can the payment be denied after success?), Info Disclosure (does routing expose user payment patterns?), DoS (can someone flood channels to denial-of-service a node?), Elevation (can a routing node escalate to access Strike's internal funds?).
  • What questions do I need answered first? What does Strike control vs. what's the Lightning Network's responsibility? Where does my threat model end and the protocol's begin?
Sample Answer

"Before we locked in the routing architecture, the conversation I'd want the team to have is about where our control actually ends. The interesting boundary isn't between us and the user — that's authenticated and rate-limited. It's between our Lightning node and the broader network, where routing nodes are third-party and untrusted. The question I'd put to the team early — as a design question, not a finding — is: can a failure mid-route leave us in a state where funds leave our node but the user's payment isn't credited? That needs to be designed against, not just tested for. I'd also want us thinking about channel management before the architecture is finalised — are there routing decisions that could expose our channel balances or payment patterns to external nodes? And what node implementation are we running? That shapes where the actual attack surface lives and I'd want to understand it while we're still making those choices."

Trap to avoid

Don't pretend you know Lightning Network internals deeply. The question is testing your framework, not your Lightning expertise. Asking what node implementation they run is a smart clarifying question — it shows you know the answer depends on specifics.

"How would you threat model our lending product — specifically the BTC collateral flow?" ▾
Lending: user deposits BTC collateral → Strike transfers it to a third-party capital provider → user receives USD loan → liquidation trigger if BTC price drops below threshold. Strike "remains legally responsible for collateral at all times."
How to think through it
  • The highest-risk boundary: BTC collateral leaving Strike's custody to the capital provider. This is an irreversible action — if something goes wrong here, Strike is legally liable.
  • What can go wrong at the handoff? Wrong destination address, manipulated transfer amount, race condition between price check and transfer execution, MITM on the API call to capital provider.
  • Liquidation logic is critical: What triggers liquidation? Who can trigger it? Can a price feed be manipulated to cause premature liquidation (oracle manipulation)? Can liquidation be delayed maliciously?
  • Trust boundary: Strike → capital provider: Is this API call authenticated? Is the capital provider's API response validated? Can the provider lie about collateral status?
  • User-side: Can a user manipulate the collateral amount reported? Can they double-spend collateral across multiple loans?
Sample Answer

"When we were designing this flow, the conversation I'd want to have before the architecture was locked is about the collateral handoff — BTC leaving our custody to the capital provider. That's the moment I'd want everyone thinking about together, because it's irreversible and we remain legally liable. As a design question rather than a finding: how are we validating the destination address before that transfer executes? Is there a race condition between the price check and the transfer? For liquidation — who owns the price feed, and have we designed against oracle manipulation? That's a known attack class in lending systems and I'd rather we talked about it at the architecture stage than after we've shipped. On the user side, I'd flag collateral double-spend as an edge case worth designing against before we write the first line of loan origination code — can someone pledge the same BTC as collateral across multiple loan positions simultaneously?"

Trap to avoid

Don't dive into generic OWASP categories. The lending product has unique risks — oracle manipulation, custody handoff integrity, liquidation logic — that don't show up in a standard web app threat model. Showing you thought about the product-specific risks is the signal.

"If you were threat modeling our authentication system, where would you start?" ▾
Strike handles real money. Account takeover = access to BTC balance, payment initiation, loan management. High-value target for credential stuffing, SIM swapping, session hijacking.
How to think through it
  • What can an attacker do with a compromised account? Send BTC, initiate a loan, change withdrawal address. That's the impact ceiling — it drives priority.
  • Entry points: Login endpoint, password reset, MFA verification, session tokens, OAuth if used, account recovery flows.
  • Highest-risk flows: Password reset (often weakest link). MFA bypass. Session fixation or hijacking after login. Trust-on-first-use if applicable.
  • What assumptions is the auth system making? That the email account isn't compromised. That the phone number isn't SIM-swapped. That rate limiting actually works under load.
  • Abuse cases beyond individual accounts: Credential stuffing at scale. Account enumeration via error messages. Timing attacks on token comparison.
Sample Answer

"The first question I'd want us to agree on as a team — before we get into implementation — is what an attacker can actually do with a compromised account. Send BTC, initiate a loan, change a withdrawal address. That impact ceiling should be driving how we design the auth system, not just how we test it. From there, the conversation I'd want to have in a design review is about which flows we're treating as the weakest link. In my experience, password reset is almost always where you're most exposed — it's where we're deliberately bypassing the primary auth mechanism, so the secondary check has to be designed carefully. I'd want us talking about SIM-swap resilience specifically, and early — not as a code review comment on a PR that's already up. Given that our users are trusting us with their Bitcoin balance, that's a design conversation worth having before the recovery flow is built."

Trap to avoid

Don't just list OWASP A07. That's a category, not an analysis. The signal is connecting the auth weaknesses to Strike's specific impact — BTC balance, payment initiation, loan access. Abstract auth talk without financial consequence framing misses the point.

02
Code Review

How would you approach a code review on [module]?

"How would you review a payment processing module for security?" ▾
Payment processing: user triggers buy/sell/send → business logic validates → transaction executed → state updated. High consequence — errors mean funds lost or incorrectly transferred.
How to think through it
  • Start with data flow, not line-by-line: Where does user input enter? Where does it reach a sensitive operation — a transfer, a database write, an external API call?
  • Authorization first: Is every payment action verified against the authenticated user's identity? Can user A initiate a payment from user B's account?
  • Business logic: Are there amount limits enforced server-side? Can someone send more than their balance through a race condition? Is decimal precision handled correctly — floating-point errors in financial math can be exploited.
  • State integrity: What happens if the external call fails mid-transaction? Is there a rollback? Can a retry cause a double payment?
  • Logging: Is the right data being logged for auditability without logging sensitive values like wallet addresses unnecessarily?
Sample Answer

"When I'm in a code review on a payment module, I'm not there to produce a findings list for the engineer to act on — I'm there to think through it with them. The first question I'd ask the engineer before I look at a single line is: what happens to the user's state if the Lightning node call fails mid-payment? If there's no clean answer to that, we've found something worth designing against before the PR merges. From there I'd want us to trace the data flow together — where does user input enter, and where does it reach something consequential? The authorisation question I'd ask explicitly: can this action only be triggered by the account that owns it? In payment code, that check being one layer too high is common and the impact is significant. I'd also ask about decimal precision directly — floating-point in financial math is a conversation worth having with the engineer, not a comment dropped in a review."

Trap to avoid

Don't say "I'd run Semgrep and see what comes up." Automated tooling is a starting point, not an answer. The CISO is asking about your analytical process — what you look for that tools miss. Business logic flaws, race conditions, and decimal precision issues won't show up in a SAST scan.

"You're reviewing an authentication endpoint — what are you looking for?" ▾
Auth endpoint: receives credentials → validates → issues session token or JWT. Critical path — failure here means account takeover.
How to think through it
  • Rate limiting: Is it enforced? At what layer? Can it be bypassed with distributed requests or header manipulation (X-Forwarded-For)?
  • Credential comparison: Is password comparison timing-safe? Timing attacks on string comparison are real.
  • Token generation: Is the session token cryptographically random? What's its entropy? How long is it valid? Is it invalidated on logout?
  • Error messages: Do failed login errors reveal whether the username exists? That's account enumeration.
  • MFA integration: Is MFA checked after password validation or before issuing the token? Can the MFA step be skipped?
  • Logging: Are failed attempts logged with enough detail for incident response without logging the password itself?
Sample Answer

"When we're reviewing an auth endpoint together, the things I want to think through with the engineer are questions I'd ask in the PR, not a checklist I run independently. Is our rate limiting applied at the right layer — or can it be bypassed by spoofing the source IP via X-Forwarded-For? That's worth confirming with the engineer rather than assuming. Is the password comparison constant-time? Worth asking explicitly. For token issuance — are we generating with a cryptographically secure source, and have we defined what events trigger invalidation? The one I'd raise as a specific comment rather than a question: error messages on failed login. If we're telling users whether the email exists versus the password is wrong, we're leaking account existence — that's a design choice worth revisiting together before it ships. And MFA sequencing — I'd want to confirm with the engineer that MFA is enforced before the token is issued, not after. That gap is surprisingly common and on our product the consequence is direct access to funds."

Trap to avoid

Don't just list vulnerability names. "I'd check for CSRF, XSS, injection" without connecting them to the specific endpoint reads as pattern matching, not thinking. Specificity — timing attacks, MFA sequencing, token invalidation — is what separates you.

03
SIEM / Alerting

What alerting would you build for our product?

"If you were setting up SIEM alerting for Strike from scratch, what would your first five rules be?" ▾
Strike has: auth system, buy/sell BTC, Lightning payments, BTC-backed lending, custody. NYDFS requires incident detection and response capability.
How to think through it
  • Prioritise by consequence: What events, if missed, cause the most damage to customers or the business?
  • Five categories: Account takeover signals, unusual payment behaviour, custody/withdrawal anomalies, lending liquidation events, infrastructure auth failures.
  • Signal vs noise: Each rule needs a threshold that catches real attacks without alert fatigue. A good alert is one someone will act on.
  • For a financial product: Volume and velocity matter. Not just "did this happen" but "did this happen at an unusual rate."
Sample Answer

"The first conversation I'd want to have isn't about rule syntax — it's about what we'd be blind to if something went wrong today. For a product like ours, the five things I'd want us to make sure we can detect are: credential stuffing patterns — both distributed IPs targeting one account and one IP targeting many, because single-IP rate limits only catch one of those; a successful login followed immediately by a payment or withdrawal, which is a strong ATO signal; a new withdrawal destination appearing for a user, especially for large amounts; anything touching the collateral transfer to our lending partners outside expected parameters — that flow is high-value and I'd want us watching it closely; and repeated MFA failures at a rate that suggests automation. I'd want to build those five as a team first, validate that each generates a signal someone would actually act on, then iterate. Alert fatigue kills detection programs — five rules that get responded to are worth more than fifty that get ignored."

Trap to avoid

Don't list generic SIEM rules from a playbook — "failed logins, privilege escalation, lateral movement." Those are enterprise network rules. Strike is a consumer financial product. The rules need to map to their specific flows — payments, custody, lending — not a corporate AD environment.

"How would you build an IR workflow for an account takeover event?" ▾
Account takeover at Strike = attacker has access to BTC balance, can initiate payments, may change withdrawal address. Time to response matters — BTC transactions are irreversible.
How to think through it
  • Speed is critical: Unlike a data breach, BTC transactions are irreversible. The IR workflow needs to be automated where possible — manual steps take too long.
  • Containment before investigation: Suspend account, revoke sessions, freeze pending withdrawals. Do this first, investigate after.
  • Evidence collection: Login history, IP addresses, device fingerprints, payment actions taken. Capture before the attacker clears sessions.
  • Communication: User notification, internal escalation, regulatory notification if funds were lost (NYDFS requirements).
  • Post-incident: How did they get in? Was this a credential stuffing success, a SIM swap, a session hijack? Root cause drives prevention.
Sample Answer

"The thing that makes our ATO response different from a normal SaaS playbook is that we can't undo a sent payment. So the workflow has to be designed around that constraint — containment happens before a human is in the loop. What I'd want us to build is an automated first response: on a high-confidence ATO signal, the account suspends, sessions revoke, and pending withdrawals hold — automatically. While that's happening we need to capture the forensic state — login IPs, device fingerprints, what actions were taken post-login — because that evidence degrades quickly if the attacker clears sessions. Then a human picks it up: what did the attacker access, was anything sent, does NYDFS notification apply? The post-incident piece I'd want us to treat as a team exercise — not to assign blame but to understand the root cause together. Whether it was credential stuffing, a SIM swap, or a session hijack determines what control we add. That's the only part that prevents the next one."

Trap to avoid

Don't build a generic IR playbook. The irreversibility of BTC is the key constraint that makes Strike's ATO IR different. If you don't mention it, you're showing you didn't think about the product.

04
WAF Rules

How would you approach WAF rules for our API?

Honest framing first: You don't have WAF rule construction experience. Say so upfront if asked directly, then pivot to what you do bring — attacker perspective, understanding of what rules need to catch, and specific Strike use cases you can reason about.
"How would you think about Cloudflare WAF rules for our buy/sell endpoints?" ▾
Buy/sell endpoints: user submits order → validated → executed against liquidity. High-frequency target for bots, scrapers, and market manipulation attempts.
How to think through it
  • What are we protecting against? Credential stuffing on auth, bot-driven price manipulation on trade endpoints, scraping of price data, API abuse at scale.
  • Rate limiting: Per IP, per authenticated user, per endpoint. Buy/sell has different risk profile than browse — tighter limits.
  • Bot scoring: Cloudflare's bot management can score requests. High bot-score traffic to trade endpoints should be challenged or blocked.
  • Geo rules: Strike is available in 100+ countries but regulated differently per jurisdiction. Geo-based rules for certain endpoints may be a compliance requirement.
  • Payload inspection: Oversized or malformed JSON payloads on API endpoints. Known injection patterns in order parameters.
Sample Answer

"I'll be straight — I haven't written Cloudflare rules directly, so I'd want to learn the tooling on the job. What I can bring is the attacker's perspective on what those rules need to catch, which I think is useful when we're deciding what to build. For our buy/sell endpoints, the patterns I'd want us protecting against are distributed credential stuffing — which single-IP rate limits don't catch — bot-driven order submission, and API abuse at scale. Before we write custom rules I'd want to understand what Cloudflare's bot management score looks like in our actual traffic, because there may be signal we're not acting on yet. And if there are jurisdictions where certain endpoints shouldn't be reachable at all, that's a geo rule that doubles as a compliance control — worth building early given the NYDFS context. The rule logic I can reason through with the team now. The syntax I'll pick up."

Trap to avoid

Don't bluff Cloudflare syntax knowledge. The CISO will probe it. The honest framing — attacker perspective, logic of what to catch, willingness to learn the tooling — is both more credible and more useful than faked familiarity.

05
RFC / Design Review

How would you engage with an RFC for a new feature?

"We're building a new feature that lets users set a recurring Bitcoin purchase. How would you engage with the RFC?" ▾
Recurring purchases: user configures schedule and amount → system automatically executes buy at interval → charges payment method → BTC added to balance. Involves stored payment credentials, scheduled jobs, and automated fund movement.
How to think through it
  • What's new about the attack surface? Stored payment credentials. Scheduled execution without user present. Long-lived authorisation tokens. All three are new risks vs. a one-time purchase.
  • Authorisation questions: Who can modify the recurring schedule? Can another user's schedule be modified via IDOR? What happens if the user's payment method changes — is the old credential cleaned up?
  • Scheduled job security: What account does the job run as? What's the blast radius if the scheduler is compromised? Can the job be triggered manually or out of schedule?
  • Cancellation and edge cases: Can a user cancel mid-execution? What's the race condition between cancellation and fund movement? Is there a log of every automated action for auditability?
  • Questions to ask in the RFC: How are stored payment credentials protected? Is there a cap on amount or frequency? What's the user notification on execution?
Sample Answer

"The way I'd engage with this RFC is to come in with questions that help the team think through a few things before the design is locked — not a list of requirements. The questions I'd bring: we're persisting payment credentials beyond a single transaction — have we agreed on how those are stored, and what happens to them if a user removes their payment method before the next execution? For the scheduler — what identity does it run as, and is it scoped to the minimum it needs? I'd want that answered in the RFC, not discovered in code review. And for the user experience — is there a notification on every execution, and can someone easily see and cancel what's queued? I'd also flag IDOR on the schedule management endpoint as something to design against explicitly: the edit and delete paths need to verify ownership, not just authentication. I'd rather that's a design note in the RFC than a comment on a merged PR."

Trap to avoid

Don't block the feature or raise generic concerns. RFC participation means being a collaborator, not a gatekeeper. The answer shows you've thought about specific risks and have specific questions — not that you want to slow things down.

06
Vuln Ownership

How do you own a vulnerability end-to-end?

"You find a critical auth vulnerability in a payment endpoint. Walk me through what you do." ▾
Critical finding in production code. Financial product. BTC at stake. Speed matters. Coordination with engineering matters more.
How to think through it
  • Validate first: Confirm it's exploitable in the actual production environment, not just in theory. Know the exact conditions needed.
  • Assess blast radius: Which accounts are affected? Has it been exploited already? Check logs before disclosing — you want to know if this is a past incident, not just a future risk.
  • Escalate appropriately: Critical on a payment endpoint goes to the CISO directly, not through a ticket queue. Verbal escalation first, written documentation immediately after.
  • Work the fix: Don't just hand it over. Sit with the engineer on the fix, validate it actually closes the vulnerability, check for the same pattern in adjacent code.
  • Close the loop: Verify in production. Update detection — add a SIEM rule or WAF rule for the exploit pattern. Document the root cause so the pattern doesn't recur.
Sample Answer

"Before I say anything to anyone, I confirm it's actually exploitable in production — not just in theory. While I'm doing that, I'm checking our logs, because if there's already evidence of exploitation we're not looking at a finding, we're in an active incident and the response changes completely. Assuming it's clean — I come to you directly, not a ticket. We talk through what I found and what the blast radius looks like together. Then I work it with the engineer, not hand it off. We sit on the fix together, I validate it closes the vulnerability rather than masking it, and we check whether the same pattern exists anywhere else in the codebase. Once it's in production, we add a detection rule for the exploit signature so we'd catch any future attempt. And we do a post-mortem as a team — not to assign blame but to understand what in our design or review process let it through. That's the only part that actually prevents the next one."

Trap to avoid

"I'd file a Jira ticket." No. Critical financial product findings get direct escalation. The CISO wants to know you understand urgency and communication, not just process compliance.
